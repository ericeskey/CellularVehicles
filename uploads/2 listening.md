Martin’s message is simple: **“I can do the science; the workflow keeps pulling me back into preventable friction.”**

## 1) What we heard

Martin is the “person in the middle” of the workflow: patient acquisition, lab work, library prep, sequencing, bioinformatics, visualization, publication, and supervision. That breadth gives him a full-system view, but it also makes preventable interruptions painful.

His strongest points:

- **MiSeq is useful and generally well-designed once things work.**
    
    - He likes the guided interface, animations, rounded design, and step-by-step loading experience.
        
- **The pain is not the core sequencing science; it is the avoidable workflow drag around it.**
    
    - Sample sheet errors are common, cryptic, and disruptive.
        
    - Denaturing is annoying, fragile, time-sensitive, and outside his real expertise.
        
    - Run time fragments the work across days.
        
    - Packaging and waste feel excessive.
        
- **Speed matters because it preserves cognitive continuity.**
    
    - He wants to review data while the experiment is still fresh in his mind.
        
- **His ideal future is radically simpler.**
    
    - “Give the machine a library, and I get my sequencing data.”
        

## 2) Said, unspoken, and not said

### Said

- He trusts the MiSeq when it works.
    
- He wants clearer error messages.
    
- He wants denaturing removed or automated.
    
- He wants shorter run times.
    
- He wants less wasteful, easier-to-open packaging.
    
- He wants more flexible FASTQ/demultiplexing output.
    

### Unspoken, but reasonably implied

- He wants the instrument to protect scarce expert attention.
    
- He wants PhD students to be more self-sufficient.
    
- He values workflow rhythm as much as raw technical capability.
    
- He sees low-throughput sequencing as a bridge between method development, QC, and high-cost NovaSeq decisions.
    

### Not said — avoid overreach

- He did **not** say MiSeq is a bad instrument.
    
- He did **not** say washing is a major pain point for him personally.
    
- He did **not** say data analysis should be fully automated.
    
- He did **not** say smaller size matters in every dimension; width matters most.
    
- He did **not** say packaging waste is his top priority; it is an irritant, not the core issue.

## 4) Decisions made

No explicit decisions were made in the interview.

The strongest implied design priorities to validate are:

1. Reduce or automate denaturing.
    
2. Improve sample sheet error diagnosis.
    
3. Shorten sequencing run time.
    
4. Preserve workflow continuity from run to analysis.
    
5. Improve output flexibility for common library/data formats.
    
6. Reduce packaging friction and waste.
    

## 5) Action items agreed to

No explicit action items were agreed to.

Natural follow-up actions:

1. Validate whether denaturing is the top pain across other users.
    
2. Test sample sheet error-message concepts with users.
    
3. Quantify the value of 4-hour, overnight, and current run-time scenarios.
    
4. Explore whether width reduction would materially change lab placement.
    
5. Probe whether packaging waste is a hygiene issue or buying-influence issue.
