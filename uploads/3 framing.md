The master challenge: **Martin does not need “better sequencing” in the abstract; he needs the workflow to stop stealing attention from the science.**

## Problem statements

1. **Preventable errors create expert dependency**
    
    - Sample sheet mistakes are common, cryptic, and solvable only when Martin intervenes.
        
    - Problem: The system fails to help non-expert users recover from routine mistakes.
        
2. **Freshness-sensitive chemistry creates workflow fragility**
    
    - Denaturing must happen right before sequencing.
        
    - Delays can force rework.
        
    - Problem: A small preparatory step carries disproportionate risk.
        
3. **Run time fragments scientific attention**
    
    - Current runs often finish too late to analyze while the experiment is fresh.
        
    - Problem: Sequencing time breaks the connection between experiment, result, interpretation, and next action.
        
4. **The instrument is physically separated from the real work**
    
    - Width and placement push the MiSeq away from the PCR/prep area.
        
    - Problem: The machine does not fit naturally into the workspace where sequencing decisions happen.
        
5. **Output flexibility lags experimental diversity**
    
    - Standard FASTQ output is expected, but different protocols require different demultiplexing needs.
        
    - Problem: The data-output layer is not flexible enough for evolving workflows.
        
6. **Packaging creates unnecessary friction and waste**
    
    - Oversized dry ice boxes, double plastic wrapping, scissors, and unclear disposal add irritation.
        
    - Problem: Consumables packaging undermines ease, sustainability, and perceived thoughtfulness.
        

## Hypotheses to test

1. **If sample sheet errors were diagnosed clearly, PhD students could self-resolve more issues and reduce Martin’s interruptions.**
    
2. **If denaturing were automated or integrated into the machine, users would perceive the workflow as dramatically safer and simpler.**
    
3. **If run time fell to four hours or reliably overnight, users would preserve experiment-to-analysis continuity and run more frequent cycles.**
    
4. **If the instrument were narrower, it could move closer to the prep workflow and reduce unnecessary movement, cold-room exposure, and rework friction.**
    
5. **If the system offered flexible preset outputs for common library types, bioinformatics-capable users would save setup time without needing full analysis automation.**
    
6. **If packaging were easier to open and less wasteful, users would read the product as more user-aware and less excessive.**
    

## Points of view

1. **The real competition is not another sequencer; it is fragmentation.**
    
    - Martin’s enemy is stop-start workflow: waiting, fixing, moving, remembering, repeating.
        
2. **The highest-value design move is to protect expert attention.**
    
    - Every avoidable interruption pulls Martin away from higher-order work.
        
3. **“Easy to do and easy to prevent” is the design brief.**
    
    - Routine errors should be caught, explained, and resolved by the system.
        
4. **Denaturing is the emotional center of the workflow pain.**
    
    - It feels low-level, fragile, outside expertise, and capable of ruining the experiment.
        
5. **Speed matters because memory decays.**
    
    - Faster turnaround is not just convenience; it preserves experimental context.
        
6. **Martin wants an instrument that behaves like a collaborator, not a checkpoint.**
    
    - His ideal is compact: “Give the machine a library, and I get my sequencing data.”
