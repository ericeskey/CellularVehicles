# When Faster Isn’t Really About Speed

## Episode Summary

Martin wants a four-hour sequencing run. The obvious assumption is that he wants more productivity.

But when he explains why faster matters, the story changes. He talks less about volume and more about memory, focus, and the ability to analyze results while the experiment is still fresh in his mind.

This episode asks: when customers ask for speed, what are they really asking for? Smart teams often measure time on the clock. Users experience something more human: whether they can carry one thought into the next action.

The discovery: speed is sometimes valuable because it protects momentum.

## In This Episode

- Why runtime is not always just an efficiency metric
    
- How delays create cognitive reentry costs
    
- Why users may want speed to preserve context, not increase volume
    
- The difference between elapsed time and experienced momentum
    
- A better question to ask when customers request “faster”
    

## Why This Matters

Teams often respond to speed requests by optimizing time. That can be useful, but it may miss the deeper reason speed matters.

If the real pain is context decay, the better design question is not only “How fast can this be?” It is “What happens to the user’s thinking while they wait?”
