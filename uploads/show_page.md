# The Hidden Cost of Broken Continuity

## About This Series

What if the real workflow problem is not the task itself, but the moments where the work loses its thread?

This series follows Martin, a translational cancer researcher in Berlin whose sequencing workflow looks, at first, like a familiar product story: errors, delays, setup friction, run times, and usability issues.

But the deeper discovery is sharper. Martin does not simply need a faster or more capable sequencer. He needs a workflow that protects scientific attention from preventable breaks.

Across five episodes, we examine how small handoffs become large sources of friction: when the machine stops but cannot explain why, when an expert becomes the workaround, when speed is really about memory, when a fragile step threatens the entire run, and when a user’s ideal future reveals what all the complaints were pointing toward.

## Episode Guide

### Episode 1 — The Hidden Cost of Broken Continuity

Martin likes his sequencer. That is why his frustration matters: the real issue may not be any single step, but the way the workflow keeps stopping between steps.

### Episode 2 — The Expert Should Not Be the Error Message

A student hits a sample-sheet error, the machine stops, and Martin becomes the interface. This episode explores how expert attention gets consumed by routine recovery work.

### Episode 3 — When Faster Isn’t Really About Speed

Martin wants a four-hour run, but not only for productivity. He wants results while the experiment is still alive in his mind.

### Episode 4 — The Fragile Handoff

The most vulnerable moment may happen before sequencing begins. This episode looks at denaturing as a small transition with outsized risk.

### Episode 5 — Give the Machine a Library

Martin’s ideal future is disarmingly simple: “Give the machine a library, and I get my sequencing data.” This final episode explores what user complaints reveal about the future they actually want.

## What You’ll Learn

- How to spot continuity loss inside complex workflows
    
- Why expert rescue can hide product or process failure
    
- Why speed often protects memory, not just throughput
    
- How small handoffs create disproportionate risk
    
- How to distinguish complaints from the future state users are reaching for
    
- Why better workflow design starts by protecting attention
    

## Recommended Starting Point

Start with Episode 1. It gives you the master lens for the series: users do not experience workflows as isolated tasks; they experience the thread between them.
