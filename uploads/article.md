# The Hidden Cost of Broken Continuity

## The Hidden Problem

Martin likes his sequencer.

That is what makes his frustration easy to misdiagnose.

He works in a translational hematology-oncology research group in Berlin. His responsibilities span patient acquisition, laboratory work, sequencing, bioinformatics, visualization, publication, and supervision. As he describes it, he is “the guy for everything.”

The instrument itself is not the source of most of his complaints. He praises its guided interface. He likes that it walks users step-by-step through setup. He appreciates the visual cues, the animations, even the industrial design.

Yet throughout the interview, a different pattern emerges.

Martin repeatedly returns to moments where the work loses momentum.

A sample sheet error forces him to leave the machine and troubleshoot elsewhere. A fresh library waits while someone tries to determine whether a delimiter is wrong. A sequencing run finishes after the context of the experiment has faded. A student encounters a familiar problem and must come find him to resolve it.

Individually, none of these moments seems significant.

Collectively, they shape how the work feels.

And, perhaps more importantly, how quickly the work advances.

## The Misleading Diagnosis

The obvious interpretation is that Martin wants a faster sequencer.

That conclusion would not be unreasonable.

He would clearly prefer shorter run times. When asked what he would change if he could, one of his first answers was to reduce run duration dramatically.

But focusing on speed alone misses something important.

Throughout the conversation, Martin rarely describes delays as isolated inconveniences. Instead, he describes them as interruptions in a larger chain of activity.

He worries about fresh material sitting idle while setup problems are resolved. He dislikes spreading work across multiple days. He wants to analyze data while the experiment that generated it is still vivid in his mind.

The issue is not merely elapsed time.

The issue is what happens when the thread connecting one step to the next is broken.

## What the Case Reveals

Several observations point toward the same underlying pattern.

Martin describes routine sample-sheet errors that are easy to make and easy to prevent. New users often encounter them. The system recognizes that something is wrong, but provides little guidance about what specifically failed.

As a result, people stop.

They come to him.

The work pauses.

“Most of the time,” he explains, the problem is one of a handful of common issues. Yet resolving those issues still requires expert attention.

A similar pattern appears around denaturing.

The procedure itself is not the most technically sophisticated part of the workflow. Yet it carries disproportionate consequences. The chemistry is time-sensitive. Conditions must be correct. Delays can force portions of the process to be repeated.

Martin's frustration is notable because it is not directed at scientific complexity.

He expects sequencing to be complex.

What bothers him is performing low-level, failure-prone tasks that exist between the science and the machine.

As he puts it, “You can easily destroy your experiment if you do something wrong there.”

The same theme surfaces again when discussing run times.

When asked why faster turnaround would matter, Martin does not primarily discuss productivity metrics.

Instead, he talks about memory.

He wants to review data while the experiment is still fresh. He wants mistakes, observations, and hypotheses to remain connected. When analysis is delayed, “things spread further, and things become more out of focus.”

The interview suggests that the real loss is not hours.

It is continuity.

## The Better Frame

This case points toward a useful reframing.

The central challenge may not be sequencing speed.

It may be continuity preservation.

Viewed through this lens, many seemingly unrelated complaints become connected.

Sample-sheet validation is not merely a usability issue. It protects momentum.

Denaturing is not merely a chemistry step. It is a fragile handoff.

Run time is not merely a performance metric. It determines whether analysis remains connected to experimentation.

Physical placement matters for the same reason. Martin notes that a narrower instrument could potentially sit closer to where preparatory work occurs. That change would not make the machine more capable. It would reduce the number of interruptions, movements, and transitions surrounding it.

The common thread is not capability.

It is the preservation of flow across handoffs.

One way to think about the problem is as a sequence of potential breaks.

Every workflow contains them. Data transfer. Approval steps. Context switches. Setup procedures. Expert reviews.

When those breaks accumulate, organizations often experience the symptoms—delays, rework, dependency on experts—without recognizing the underlying cause.

The work is no longer moving as a continuous system.

It is moving in fragments.

## Why It Matters Beyond This Case

This case does not prove that continuity is the dominant challenge in every sequencing workflow.

Nor does it suggest that speed, usability, automation, or instrument performance are unimportant.

However, it raises a broader question that extends well beyond sequencing.

How often do organizations mistake capability problems for continuity problems?

Product teams frequently respond to customer requests by adding features.

Operations teams invest in efficiency.

Technology groups focus on throughput.

Yet users often experience work as a series of connected moments rather than isolated functions.

A system that is technically powerful but repeatedly breaks continuity may feel slower and harder to use than a less capable system that preserves momentum.

The distinction is subtle but important.

One approach optimizes individual steps.

The other protects the connections between them.

## What Leaders Should Look For

Leaders attempting to identify similar patterns should ask a different set of questions.

Rather than asking:

- Where are tasks slow?
    
- Which features are missing?
    
- Which steps require the most effort?
    

Ask:

- Where does work stop unexpectedly?
    
- Which handoffs require expert intervention?
    
- Where does context decay between action and feedback?
    
- What information must people repeatedly remember instead of the system remembering it?
    
- Which routine mistakes remain preventable but unresolved?
    
- Where does momentum get lost?
    

The answers may reveal bottlenecks that traditional process metrics overlook.

## The Shift

At first glance, Martin appears to be describing a collection of unrelated frustrations: sample sheets, denaturing, run times, instrument size, packaging, and data output.

By the end of the conversation, those complaints begin to look different.

They are not separate issues.

They are manifestations of the same underlying challenge.

Martin is trying to preserve a thread.

From preparation to sequencing. From sequencing to analysis. From analysis to the next experiment.

Every interruption weakens that thread.

His ideal future is remarkably simple.

“Give the machine a library, and I get my sequencing data.”

Not because complexity disappears.

But because the continuity survives.

The image that remains is not a faster machine.

It is a scientist carrying an experiment from one stage to the next without having to stop and reconstruct it along the way.

## The Managerial Takeaway

The old assumption is that workflow improvement comes primarily from increasing capability—more speed, more features, more automation. This case suggests a different frame: the most consequential failures often occur at the handoffs between steps, where continuity is lost and expert attention is consumed. Leaders should look beyond individual tasks and examine where momentum breaks. Improving those transitions may create more value than optimizing any single step in isolation.
