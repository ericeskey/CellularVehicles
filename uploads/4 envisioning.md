The vision: **Martin gets a sequencer workflow that preserves scientific focus instead of fragmenting it.**

## What are we aiming for?

A low-throughput sequencing experience where Martin can:

1. **Give the machine a prepared library and get usable sequencing data**
    
    - Minimal fragile chemistry.
        
    - Minimal expert-only troubleshooting.
        
    - Minimal rework.
        
2. **Keep the workflow in one mental frame**
    
    - Prep, run, review, and next-step decisions stay connected.
        
    - Results arrive while the experiment is still fresh.
        
3. **Let capable non-experts succeed without Martin rescuing them**
    
    - PhD students can resolve common sample sheet issues themselves.
        
    - The machine explains what went wrong and what to do next.
        
4. **Make the physical workflow fit the lab**
    
    - The instrument and needed materials sit near the work.
        
    - Width, placement, water, reagents, fridge/freezer access, and centrifuge needs are integrated.
        

## What will it look like when they get there?

Martin’s future-state day looks like this:

- A student prepares a library without needing Martin to check routine setup mistakes.
    
- The machine validates the sample sheet before the fresh library is at risk.
    
- Denaturing is either removed, automated, or made nearly foolproof.
    
- The run finishes in a time window that supports same-day or next-morning analysis.
    
- Output options match common library types without manual workaround.
    
- Martin reviews data while the experiment is still cognitively alive.
    
- The next run can be planned quickly, without the workflow spreading across a week.
    

The emotional signal: **less “I’m the guy for everything,” more “the system lets my team work.”**

## How will we know we are there?

Evidence markers:

1. **Fewer Martin interruptions**
    
    - Students resolve common sample sheet errors independently.
        
2. **Less rework**
    
    - Denaturing does not need to be repeated because of preventable setup delays.
        
3. **Shorter cycle time**
    
    - Run-to-analysis-to-next-decision happens overnight or within one working day.
        
4. **Higher workflow continuity**
    
    - Martin can connect result, mistake analysis, and next preparation while details are fresh.
        
5. **More frequent runs**
    
    - The lab can move from fragmented weekly cycles toward daily or every-other-day rhythm when needed.
        
6. **Lower cognitive load**
    
    - Martin spends less time remembering fixes, gathering chemicals, and managing fragile handoffs.
        

## The chosen future state

Not “a better MiSeq.”

**A self-protecting sequencing workflow:** one that catches preventable errors early, absorbs fragile prep steps, shortens feedback loops, and lets Martin’s attention stay on the science.
