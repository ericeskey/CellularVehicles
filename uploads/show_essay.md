# The Hidden Cost of Broken Continuity

## The Case

Martin spends his days moving between worlds.

Some of his time is spent with patients and clinical research. Some is spent preparing sequencing libraries. Some is spent analyzing data. Some is spent helping students navigate unfamiliar procedures. In his own telling, he has become “the guy for everything.”

The sequencing instrument at the center of this story is not a villain. Martin speaks positively about many aspects of it. The interface is thoughtful. The workflow is guided. The machine generally does what it is supposed to do.

Yet over the course of a long conversation, a different kind of frustration emerges. Small interruptions keep appearing. A sample sheet error. A troubleshooting detour. A delay before analysis can begin. A preparation step that turns a stable process into a fragile one. Each interruption seems minor when viewed on its own. Together, they shape how the work feels.

By the end of the interview, the sequencing machine itself is no longer the most interesting part of the story.

## The Problem Beneath the Problem

Many workflow conversations begin with visible symptoms.

Users ask for faster processing. Better interfaces. More automation. Fewer errors. Shorter run times.

Martin mentioned all of these things at one point or another. Taken individually, they sound like ordinary requests. A product team could easily translate them into a backlog of improvements and begin prioritizing features.

Listening closely, however, reveals a pattern that is harder to see from a list of requirements.

A sequencing run finishes long after the experiment that produced it has faded from memory. A student encounters a routine error and must seek help from an expert. A preparation step introduces time pressure and risk. Attention shifts. Context disappears. Momentum has to be rebuilt.

The pattern points toward something broader than usability or efficiency. Work is repeatedly losing continuity.

That observation changes the meaning of many of Martin’s complaints. A faster run is valuable partly because it preserves context. Better error handling matters because it prevents unnecessary interruption. Certain preparation steps feel burdensome because they create vulnerable transitions between states.

Viewed this way, the workflow resembles a thread being carried through a series of handoffs. The challenge is not simply moving forward. The challenge is avoiding the moments when the thread slips from your hands.

## What The Series Explores

This series begins with that thread.

The opening episode examines continuity itself: why workflows that appear functional can still feel fragmented to the people who rely on them. From there, attention shifts to a less obvious consequence. Whenever continuity breaks, someone is usually responsible for repairing it. In Martin’s environment, that person is often the expert.

The discussion then moves into time. A request for speed sounds straightforward until you hear Martin explain why faster matters. His answer has less to do with productivity than memory, focus, and the ability to remain connected to the work.

Later episodes examine a single preparation step that carries disproportionate risk and explore what it can teach us about fragile transitions. The series concludes by stepping back from individual frustrations and asking a different question altogether: what future is the user actually trying to create?

Across the five episodes, the focus gradually widens. What begins as a story about sequencing becomes an exploration of workflow design, expert attention, human cognition, and the hidden structure of complex work.

## Why It Matters

One interview does not establish a universal truth. Martin’s experience belongs to a specific setting, with its own technologies, constraints, and practices.

Even so, the case raises a question that extends well beyond sequencing.

Organizations tend to evaluate workflows through the lens of activities. They measure tasks, optimize steps, and improve components. Users experience something more continuous. They live inside the spaces between activities, where context must be carried forward and where interruptions accumulate.

Many operational problems become easier to understand once those spaces come into view.

A recurring delay may be less important than the effort required to recover from it. A routine error may matter less for its immediate impact than for the attention it repeatedly consumes. A request for speed may reflect a desire to preserve focus rather than increase output.

Martin’s story offers a way of seeing those patterns more clearly.

## What To Listen For

As you listen to the series, pay attention to a few recurring questions.

Where does work stop unexpectedly?

Who is responsible for getting it moving again?

What happens to a person’s thinking while they wait?

Which transitions create the most vulnerability?

And beneath every complaint, what future is the user trying to reach?

Those questions appear in different forms throughout the series. Together, they create a lens for understanding workflows that extends far beyond this particular case.

## Closing

Near the end of the interview, Martin is asked what he would change if he could change anything.

After discussing errors, preparation steps, run times, packaging, software, and laboratory logistics, his answer becomes surprisingly simple.

“Give the machine a library, and I get my sequencing data.”

The statement is memorable because of everything it leaves out.

Behind it sits an entire workflow filled with decisions, handoffs, delays, dependencies, and interruptions. The series begins inside that complexity. It ends by asking what remains when all of the unnecessary distance is stripped away.

Somewhere between those two points lies the hidden cost of broken continuity.
